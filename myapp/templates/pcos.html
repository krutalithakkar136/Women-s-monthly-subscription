PCOS
(POLYCYSTIC OVARY SYNDROME)

If you have PCOS, you may not have periods very often. Or you may have periods that last many days. You may also have too much of a hormone called androgen in your body. With PCOS, many small sacs of fluid develop along the outer edge of the ovary. These are called cysts. The small fluid-filled cysts contain immature eggs. These are called follicles. The follicles fail to regularly release eggs.

Symptoms of PCOS often start around the time of the first menstrual period. Sometimes symptoms develop later after you have had periods for a while. The symptoms of PCOS vary. A diagnosis of PCOS is made when you have at least two of these: Irregular periods, Too much androgen, and Polycystic ovaries.

Complications of PCOS can include: (1)Infertility, (2)Gestational diabetes or pregnancy-induced high blood pressure, (3)Miscarriage or premature birth, Nonalcoholic steatohepatitis — a severe liver inflammation caused by fat buildup in the liver, (4)Metabolic syndrome — a cluster of conditions including high blood pressure, (5)high blood sugar and unhealthy cholesterol or triglyceride levels that significantly increase your risk of heart and blood vessel (cardiovascular) disease, (6)Type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, (7)Sleep apnea, Depression, (8)anxiety and eating disorders, and (9)Cancer of the uterine lining (endometrial cancer).
